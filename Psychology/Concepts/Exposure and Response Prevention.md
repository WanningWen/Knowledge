> [!important] individuals are repeatedly exposed to their obsession, thus causing anxiety/fears, while simultaneously prevented from engaging in their compulsive behaviors. 

Exposure sessions are often done _in vivo_ (in real life), via videos, or even imaginary. 
	For example:
	a fear that one’s house would burn down if their compulsion was not carried out would obviously be done via imaginary exposure, as it would not be ethical to have a person burn their house down.
### History:
First developed by psychiatrist Victor Meyer (1966)
### Process:
1. the clinician must teach the patient relaxation techniques for them to engage in during the distress of being exposed to the obsession
2. develop a hierarchy of obsessions
3. start at those with the lowest amount of distress
	For example:
	an individual obsessed with germs, may first watch a person sneeze on the computer in session; move on to being present in the same room as a sick individual; shaking hands with someone obviously sick.
	Then move on to the next obsession and so forth until the entire list was complete.
### Outcome:
- some studies suggest up to an 86% response rate when treatment is completed (Foa et al., 2005)
- combination with family counseling using [[Cognitive Behavioral Therapy|CBT]] may increase this response rate even higher (Bolleau, 2011; Krebs & Heyman, 2015)
- the largest barrier to treatment is getting patients to commit to treatment, as the repeated exposures and prevention of compulsive behaviors can be extremely distressing to patients
