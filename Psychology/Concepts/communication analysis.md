> [!important] an [[Interpersonal psychotherapy|IPT]] strategy that helps reveal issues, problems and opportunities for adaptive changes in close relationships
> - use when there's an upsetting relational interaction
> - so patient want to & use alternative approaches to interacting.
> - take a deeper look at the interchange so they can explore the motives of the other, their own blind spots, and their fears
## Goals:
- clarify the details of this specific conversation
- elicit specifics of what was said and how the communication unfolded
- link this relational interaction and the patient’s distress
- explore the emotions, intentions, expectations, impacts and perspectives of the patient and the other with whom they had the interaction
- brainstorm to generate ideas for a future conversation, often with revised expectations, empathy and clearer expression
- practice using role play as required
- patient can better recognize the need to consider alternative approaches to interacting.
## problematic communication patterns
- assuming one is understood or understands others, when little is actually said
- unclear or hostile communication that frightens, angers, confuses or is off-putting to others
### Causes:
Difference between:
- what is said
- how it is said
- what is left unsaid
- what is understood, inferred or assumed
about:
- others’ intentions, needs, feelings, and inadvertent, unintended impacts upon others.
### Factors shaping communication:
- social determinants of health that relate to one’s sense of agency, safety, power or influence
- temperamental factors such as shyness
- early childhood experiences and unresolved developmental [[Trauma]]
- psychological traits or personality features
	- conscientiousness, agreeableness, openness, neuroticism (emotional reactivity), or extraversion
- emotional states
	- such as anger or fear, when dysregulated or at a high level of intensity that interfere with the ability to think and communicate
- cultural factors that shape expectations and impose taboos
- [[attachment theory|attachment styles]] or habitual patterns 
	- based on [[Psychological Modeling]], past experiences and expectations of others.
## Methods:
1. explore the details of an emotionally charged interaction
	- what and how things are said, felt, intended, understood, expected and responded to
	- Compare what was actually said (e.g., what words were used) to what the patient wanted to be understood
2. patients present only the most heated or upsetting moment of an interaction
3. rewind and go back to the beginning of the conversation and learn about what happened in detail in order to appreciate the context, perspectives or needs of each of the parties
	- Raise empathic awareness of how others may be affected by the communication
	- when communication is vague or not easily understood
	- analyze the expectations that the patient has of others
		- evaluate whether these are both realistic and reasonable
### Questions:
- What did you say?
- How did they respond?
- What was going on with your (or their) feelings?
### Mentalizing
- What the patient thinks may be in the minds and experiences of others.
- Why did you think they did/said that?
- What do you make of that?
- How did you feel? How do you imagine the other felt?
- What do you think you could have said/ would have said to better help [other person] to understand/hear that wish for [expectations]?
	- how do you think she might respond if you were to say things in that way
