> [!important] a state of ==harmonious understanding with another individual or group== that enables ==greater and easier communication==. 

In other words, rapport is ==getting along well with another person, or group of people, by having things in common==. This makes the communication process easier and usually more effective

opens the door to a variety of perspectives for clinicians
- Memory Difficulties
- Frustrations
- Embarrassments
- Side-effects
- Fears
### [[Be Sensitive Be Brave]] : To Build Rapport...
^e8ac15
1. Show them that you care about and notice what they're going through:
	"I notice that you've been having a hard time."
2. Repeat what you heard: 
	"So it sounds like..."
3. Respond with [[Empathy|empathy]]: 
	"That sounds really difficult."
4. Acknowledge the difficulty of sharing:
	"I can imagine that sharing this is difficult."
5. Do not question the reality of their pain just because you cannot see it, instead say:
	"This must be painful for you."
## Requirements:
Bordin’s (1979) three-part conceptualization：
1) agreement between patient and therapist on the tasks of treatment
	- ([[Interpersonal psychotherapy|IPT]] e.g., to improve symptoms and functioning)
2) agreement on the goals of treatment
	- in [[Interpersonal psychotherapy|IPT]] , treatment tasks are determined by the interpersonal focus or problem area.
3) a personal, affective bond between the patient and therapist
	- patients feel understood and attended to by their therapist

### [[alliance rupture]]


### Repair:
Alliance repair strategies (Safran, Muran & Eubanks-Carter, 2011):
- Validate patients’ emotions. Notice and explore when patients are upset or disengaged.
- Revisit treatment goals to clarify or modify if needed.
- Restore the bond. 
	- Pay attention to gaps between therapist intentions and impacts on the patient. 
	- Explore and accept responsibility for distress, hurt feelings, or misunderstandings.
