> [!important] 
> Disorders characterized by one or more of the following: delusions, hallucinations, disorganized thinking and speech, disorganized motor behavior, and negative symptoms ([[DSM V]])
## Clinical Presentation
- varies a lot between individuals: [[heterogeneous clinical syndrome]] (APA, 2022).
at least two of the following during a one month period: 
#symptoms
- [[delusion]]
- [[hallucinations]]
- disorganized speech
- disorganized/abnormal behavior
- [[negative symptoms]] 
Causes:
- significant impairment in an individual’s ability to engage in normal daily functioning such as work, school, relationships with others, or self-care
- continuous signs of the disturbance **persist for at least 6 months**
 “subthreshold” forms of psychotic symptoms:
- **[[prodromal symptoms]]** precede the active phase of the disorder
- **[[residual symptoms]]** that follow it.
Mood disorder symptoms:
- psychotic features will exist beyond the remission of depressive symptoms
### Phases:
#### 1. Prodrome
beginning of symptoms, less severe, not apparent to others
- withdraw from friends and family
- functioning becomes deteriorating 
if caught early, treatment can began here
#### 2. Active
- significant and dramatic symptoms
- diagnosis given, treatment begins
#### Residual:
- reduction in positive symptoms, some negative symptoms remain
- improved functioning
- 25% recover completely, remaining struggle with symptoms rest of their lives
### History:
- formally identified and ​described in 1899 by ​a German psychiatrist named Emil Kraepelin (as dementia praecox)
- ​In 1908, Kraeplin's colleague ​Eugen Bleuler first used the term schizophrenia (greek for split and mind) 
	- root of the disorder to be: inability to associate ​one thought to another thought in ​a way we would consider logical and understandable
## Epidemiology/ Prevalence
- 1 in 100 (2.4 million) American adults live with schizophrenia (Schizophrenia. (n.d.). Retrieved January 16, 2015,)
- approximately 0.3%-0.7% of the general population (APA, 2022)
- typically occurs between late teens and mid-30s
### gender:
- equal risk for both genders
- men: more [[negative symptoms]]
- women: more affect-laden symptoms (lack of emotions, etc.)
- slightly earlier onset for males than females (APA, 2022)
### prognosis
##### onset:
- between late teens and mid-30s
	 - Earlier onset of the disorder is generally predictive of a worse overall prognosis
- gradual onset symptoms (similar to [[Major Depressive Disorder]])
- [[negative symptoms]] are more predictive of prognosis (more persistent and difficult to treat)
### Recovery:
-  estimated 13.5% of individuals diagnosed
	- data from one meta-analysis of 50 studies of individuals with broadly defined schizophrenia (APA, 2022).

## Etiology
### biological causes 
#### Genetic influences. 
Twin and Family studies:
- if one identical twin develops schizophrenia, there is a 48% chance that the other will also develop the disorder within their lifetime (Coon & Mitter, 2007)
- 17% in fraternal twins
- 281 ==gene defects==
#### Neurobiological structures. 
- reduction in overall and specific brain region volumes & tissue density compared to healthy controls (Brugger, & Howes, 2017)
- ventricle enlargement & volume reductions in the **medial temporal lobe** 
	- amygdala (involved in emotion regulation)
	- the hippocampus (involved in memory)
	- neocortical surface of the temporal lobes (processing of auditory information)
- reduction in the **orbitofrontal regions** (response inhibition) (Kurtz, 2015)
##### Dopamine Hypothesis: (too much dopamine)
neurons using dopamine fire too often
How it was hypothesized:
-  phenothiazine (antihistamine to treat allergies) improving schizophrenia
- Parkinson's disease treatment (L-Dopa) raises dopamine, too much L-Dopa causes psychotic symptoms
- amphetamines also develops psychosis 

#### stress cascade
==stressful precipitating factor== triggers developing the disorder
- [[hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal axis]] mediates stress 
	- corticotropin-releasing hormone [CRH]
	- adrenocorticotropic hormone [ACTH]
	- glucocorticoids [cortisol] -- fight or flight
>  abnormal brain structures, persistently increased levels of glucocorticoids in brain structures may be the key to the onset of psychosis in prodromal patients (Corcoran et al., 2003)
### psychological causes
#### cognitive: 
[[diathesis-stress model]]
1. premorbid neurocognitive impairment
2. places individuals at risk for aversive work/academic/interpersonal experiences
3. dysfunctional beliefs and cognitive appraisals
4. maladaptive behaviors ([[Knowledge/Psychology/Courses/hallucinations|hallucinations]], [[delusion]]s)
##### Misinterpretation hypothesis: biology + learning
[[delusion]]s and [[hallucinations]] worsen when people try to assign meanings to them or makes sense of them and withdraw from family/friends who do not confirm their reality
### sociocultural causes
Expressed emotion
- families high in expressed emotion (high hostile, critical, or overinvolved family members), are predictors of relapse (Bebbington & Kuipers, 2011).
	- individuals who return post-hospitalization to families with high criticism and emotional involvement are twice as likely to relapse compared to those who return to families with low expressed emotion (Corcoran et al., 2003).
- one major treatment goal: reduce expressed emotion within family interactions.
- Having schizophrenia diagnosed family member --> increase disruptive family environment (Friedrich et al., 2015).
- families with a loved one diagnosed with schizophrenia often report more conflict in the home as well as more difficulty communicating with one another (Kurtz, 2015).
#### better outcomes in developing countries -> better family and community groups
- possibly due to better family and social supports

# [[Mental Health Treatment]]
> [!NOTE] requires lifelong treatment and care.
> Rarely do a combination of psychopharmacological, psychological, and family interventions restore a patient to premorbid levels of functioning (Kurtz, 2015; Penn et al., 2004). 
## [[psychoactive medications|Medications]]
symptoms reduced and stabilized through medication -> participation and benefit from psychotherapy
### Phenothiazines(First generation antipsychotic medication)
- reduce positive symptoms
- no impact on negative symptoms (allogia, avolition)
- reduce symptoms but don't treat them
Example: [[Thorazine]] 1954
- affect dopamine receptors
- derivative of antihistamines
- calming effect on even the most severely agitated patients and allowed for the organization of thought
#### side effects
  [[extra parameter side effect]]s (bodily movements)
	- psychotic symptoms were replaced with muscle tremors, involuntary movements, and muscle rigidity
Akathisia:
- extreme ​restlessness, discomfort in the arms and legs and an inability to remain still.
 Severe: **[[tardive dyskinesia]]**
	- 15% of people, more often the longer a patient is on medication
		- clinicians are careful not exceeding the clinically effective dose of conventional antipsychotic medications
	- Continues after the medication discontinues, never fully disappears
	- alternative medications are often added to produce a synergistic effect (Roh et al., 2014)
Sedation, drowsiness, significant weight gain
### _second-generation_ or _atypical_ antipsychotic drugs
> [!NOTE] effectiveness and minimal side effects
- act on both dopamine and serotonin receptors
- common medications such as clozapine (Clozaril), risperidone (Risperdal), and aripiprazole (Abilify), appear to be more effective in managing both positive and negative symptoms.
- lower [[tardive dyskinesia]] than conventional antipsychotic medications
- atypical antipsychotic medications are typically the first line of treatment for schizophrenia (Barnes & Marder, 2011).
#### clozapine (Clozaril)
-  1 to 1 .5% risk of causing a granuloma psychosis (a sudden drop in white blood cells that can be fatal)
#### Zyprexa
significant weight gain, type 2 diabetes
## [[Cognitive Behavioral Therapy|CBT]]
- focuses on the maladaptive emotional and behavioral responses to psychotic experiences, which is directly related to distress and disability. 
- Therefore, the goal of CBT is ==not on symptom reduction==, but rather to improve the interpretations and understandings of these symptoms (and experiences) which will reduce associated distress (Kurtz, 2015). 
### components:
1. **psychoeducation** about their disease and the course of their symptoms (i.e., ways to identify coming and going of delusions/hallucinations)
2. **challenging** and replacing the **negative thoughts/behaviors** associated with their delusions/hallucinations to more positive thoughts/behaviors
3. positive coping strategies to deal with their unpleasant symptoms
 (Veiga-Martinez, Perez-Alvarez, & Garcia-Montes, 2008)

### results:
 One study conducted by Aaron Beck (the founder of CBT) and colleagues (Grant, Huh, Perivoliotis, Stolar, & Beck, 2011): recovery-oriented CBT produced a marked improvement in overall functioning as well as symptom reduction in patients diagnosed with schizophrenia. 
 - focusing on targeted goals (independent living, securing employment, and improving social relationships)
 - CBT strategies: role-playing, scheduling community outings, and addressing negative cognitions --> address cognitive and social skill deficits.
## Family Therapy: 
based on the *diathesis-stress model* of schizophrenia (enviornmental stressors & psychological factors exacerbate symptoms --> impact illness presentation (Haddock & Spaulding, 2011))
> [!NOTE] Goal: reduce stress on the individual 
> no specific outline but shown to reduce re-hospitalization rates, as well as slow the worsening of schizophrenia-related symptoms (Pitschel-Walz, Leucht, Baumi, Kissling, & Engel, 2001).
### Components:
#### psychoeducation
on both patient and family members (more than half of those recovering from a psychotic episode reside with their family (Haddock & Spaulding, 2011))
- the course of the illness
- recognize onset of psychotic symptoms
#### problem-solving skills
- family members learn problem solving skills to address family conflicts
- positive coping strategies for dealing with the symptoms
#### [[Cognitive Behavioral Therapy|CBT]]
reduce negativity among family member interactions
## Social Therapy/Social Skills Training
- Impaired ability to interact with individuals in a social, occupational, or recreational setting is related to poorer psychological adjustment (Bellack, Morrison, Wixted, & Mueser, 1990)
What individuals learn:
- role-play in a group therapy setting: how to interact with others appropriately (e.g., establish eye contact, engage in reciprocal conversations, etc.)
## inpatient/ community based treatments
_Short-term_ hospitalizations (few weeks)
- modify antipsychotic medications
- implement additional psychological treatments (for safely returning home)
_partial_ hospitalizations (a full-day program but returns home for the evening)
- intensive therapy
- organized activities
- group therapy programs that enhance social skills training
Research supports the use of partial hospitalizations as individuals enrolled in these programs tend to do better than those who enter outpatient care (Bales et al., 2014).
