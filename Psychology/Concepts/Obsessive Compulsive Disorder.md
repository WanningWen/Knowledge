> [!info] requires [[obsession]], [[compulsion]] or both
> behaviors (compulsions) used to neutralize the thoughts (obsessions) provide temporary relief to the individual
## Clinical Presentation
## Epidemiology/ Prevalence
- approximately 1.2% both in the U.S. and worldwide (APA, 2022)
- women more often; boys are diagnosed more frequently than girls
	- females more cleaning related obsessions and compulsions
	- males more forbidden thoughts and symmetry (APA, 2022).
## [[comorbidity]]
- Nearly 76% of individuals with OCD will be diagnosed with another [[Anxiety Disorders]]
	- commonly panic disorder, social anxiety disorder, generalized anxiety disorder, or a specific phobia.
- nearly 41% of those with OCD will also be diagnosed with a [[Depressive Disorders|depression|depressive]] or [[Bipolar Disorders| bipolar disorder]] (APA, 2022)
- tic disorder, particularly in males with an onset of OCD in childhood
	-  a strong triad of OCD, tic disorder, and ADHD in children
- Routine assessment for OCD in these disorders:
	- [[Schizophrenia]], [[Bipolar Disorders|bipolar]] disorder, [[Eating Disorders]], [[Body Dysmorphic Disorder]], and Tourette’s disorder
### Suicide:
- mean rate of lifetime suicide attempts of 14.2%
- lifetime suicidal ideation of 44.1%
- current suicidal ideation of 25.9%

- Predictors for greater [[Suicide]] risk:
	- severity of OCD
	- symptom dimension of unacceptable thoughts
	- history of suicidality
	- severity of comorbid depressive and anxiety symptoms
## Etiology
### biological causes 
#### **Hereditary transmission**
##### Genetics:
- monozygotic twins have a substantially greater concordance rate (80-87%) than dizygotic twins (47-50%; Carey & Gottesman, 1981; van Grootheest, Cath, Beekman, & Boomsma, 2005)
- first degree relatives of patients diagnosed with OCD are at a 5-fold increase to develop OCD at some point throughout their lifespan (Nestadt, et al., 2000).
- family members of individuals with OCD had higher rates of both [[obsession]] and [[compulsion]] than control families (Nestadt and colleagues, 2000)
	- stronger heritability association for [[obsession]] than [[compulsion]]
##### Age of onset:
- Individuals who experienced an earlier age of onset, particularly before age 17, were found to have more first-degree relatives diagnosed with OCD.
- no relationship between family diagnoses after the age of 17
	- those who develop OCD at an older age may have a different diagnostic origin (Nestadt, et al., 2000).
#### Neurobiological structures
##### Neurotransmitters:
[[serotonin]] have been identified as a contributing factor
- comorbid patients accidentally given [[Antidepressant]]s (clomipramine and fluoxetine) report a substantial improvement in their OCD symptoms (Bokor & Anderson, 2014)
	- Antidepressant medications that do not affect serotonin levels are _not_ effective in managing obsessive and compulsive symptoms (Sinopoli, Burton, Kronenberg, & Arnold, 2017; Bokor & Anderson, 2014)
[[glutamate]], [[GABA]], and [[dopamine]] --> development and maintenance of OCD but no definitive conclusions (Marinova, Chuang, & Fineberg, 2017).
##### Brain structures
 individuals with obsessive compulsive behaviors experience ==overactivity== of the [[orbitofrontal cortex]] and a lack of filtering in the [[caudate nuclei]], thus causing too many impulses to transfer to the [[thalamus]] (Endrass et al., 2011).
 - damage to the orbitofrontal cortex or caudate nuclei and experience remission of OCD symptoms (Hofer et al., 2013)
#### Cognitive
- overestimate the probability of harm, loss of control, or uncertainty in their life --> over-interpret potential negative outcomes of events
- [[disconfirmation bias]]
- inability to trust themselves and their instincts --> feel the need to repeat the compulsive behavior multiple times to ensure it is done correctly
- more intrusive thoughts (Jacob, Larson, & Storch, 2014)
	- unable to neutralize these thoughts without performing a ritual as a way to put themselves at ease
	- repeatedly engages in the compulsive behaviors to neutralize their anxiety, the behavior is repeatedly reinforced, thus becoming a compulsion
	- using more neutralizing strategies and report significant reductions in anxiety after employing these neutralizing techniques (Jacob, Larson, & Storch, 2014; Salkovskis et al., 2003).
### psychological causes
#### Behavioral
compulsions begin with and are maintained through **[[operant conditioning]]**
1. negative thoughts or anxieties related to an unpleasant event (obsession; the event is a stimulus)
2. cause significant distress to the individual
3. seek out some behavior (compulsion; the response) to alleviate these threats (i.e., escape behavior associated with negative reinforcement)
4. temporary relief -> reinforcing the compulsive behaviors used to lessen the threat
 behavioral treatment option: among the most effective treatments for these disorders

## [[psychoactive medications|psychopharmacology]]
Among the most effective medications are those that inhibit the reuptake of serotonin: [[clomipramine]] and [[Selective serotonin reuptake inhibitors|SSRI]]s
- Reportedly, up to 60% of patients show improvement in symptoms while taking these medications; however, symptoms are quick to return when medications are discontinued (Dougherty, Rauch, & Jenike, 2002).
-  the inclusion of medication in treatment does not provide an added benefit (Foa et al., 2005).
