> [!important] intense fear or anxiety triggered by two or more of the following because they believe something terrible may occur and due to concern over not being able to escape or help not being available (APA, 2022)
> - using public transportation such as planes, trains, ships, buses
> - being in large, open spaces such as parking lots or on bridges
> - being in enclosed spaces like stores or movie theaters
> - being in a crowd or standing in line
> - or being outside of the home alone

^ded986
^bd1ace
The avoidance can result in the person being homebound. The fear or anxiety is out of proportion to the actual danger they pose and has been present for at least six months.
## epidemiology/prevalence
- Prevalence: ^01711d
	- adolescents and adults is 1% to 1.7%. As with other anxiety disorders
	- women are twice as likely to be diagnosed with it
	- aged 65 and up adults in the United States have a 12-month prevalence of 0.4% 
	- aged 55 and up in Europe and North America, the prevalence is 0.5%.
 ^e9f4e4
## comorbidity
- other anxiety disorders and [[Depressive Disorders|depressive disorders]]
- PTSD and alcohol use disorder
- agoraphobia is more treatment-resistant for those with comorbid [[Major Depressive Disorder|major depressive disorder]]
- About 15% of patients diagnosed with agoraphobia report suicidal thoughts or behavior (APA, 2022)
## Treatment
### individuals WITH panic symptoms
[[Exposure treatments]] are less effective in providing complete relief from the disorder
- success rate may be impacted by the high comorbidity rate of agoraphobia and [[Panic Disorder]]
- residual panic symptoms often remain (Craske & Barlow, 2014)
### individuals WITHOUT panic symptoms:
individual [[Exposure treatments]] and group therapy: successful improvement for nearly 60 to 80 percent of patients with agoraphobia; but relatively high partial [[relapse]] 
- group: exposure-based field trips to various community locations, also maintaining a sense of support and security from a group of individuals whom they know
- long-term treatment or booster sessions should continue for several years at minimum (Craske & Barlow, 2014).
