## Prevalence/ Epidemiology
- 2.6% (61.1 million) of American adults live with bipolar disorder. (Source: Bipolar Disorder Among Adults.(n.d.). Retrieved January 16, 2015)
Bipolar I:
- 12-month prevalence in the United States is 1.5% and did not differ statistically between men and women (APA, 2022)
Bipolar II disorder:
-  0.8% in the United States and 0.3% internationally (APA, 2022)
- some clinical samples suggest it is more common in women, with approximately 80-90% of individuals with rapid-cycling episodes being women (Bauer & Pfenning, 2005)
-  Childbirth may be a specific trigger for a [[hypomanic episode]]: in 10-20% of women most often in the early postpartum period.
## Bipolar I vs. Bipolar II
> [!NOTE]
> - Diagnosis bipolar I disorder …. if an individual has ever experienced a **manic** episode
> - Diagnosis bipolar II disorder … if the criteria has only been met for a **hypomanic** episode
> 	-an individual must report symptoms consistent with a major depressive episode _and_ at least one [[hypomanic episode]].

## Etiology:
### Biology:
#### Genetics:
*twin studies* (identical twins vs fraternal twins):
- [[Bipolar Disorders]]: identical twins at as high as 72%; fraternal twins, siblings, and other close relatives ranged 5-15%.
	- both percentages are significantly higher than that of the general population. strong genetic component within bipolar disorder (Edvardsen et al., 2008)
	- [[DSM-5-TR]] more recently reports heritability estimates around 90% in some twin studies and the risk of bipolar disorder being around 1% in the general population compared to 5-10% in a first-degree relative (APA, 2022).
#### Biochemical Deficit
- low levels of serotonin and _high levels_ of norepinephrine may explain mania episodes (Soreff & McInnes, 2014).
#### Brain Anatomy:
- [[basal ganglia]] and [[cerebellum]] appear to be much smaller in individuals
- decrease in brain activity in regions associated with regulating emotions, as well as an increase in brain activity among structures related to emotional responsiveness (Houenou et al., 2011)
## Treatment
### [[psychoactive medications]] / Psychopharmacology
> [!NOTE] controversy
1.  aggressively with [[mood stabilizers]]
	less potent in treating depressive symptoms, and therefore, the combination approach is believed to help manage both the manic and depressive episodes (Nivoli et al., 2011).
2. treat symptoms with newer antidepressants early in treatment
	 large scale research studies have not shown great support for this method (Gijsman, Geddes, Rendell, Nolen, & Goodwin, 2004; Moller, Grunze & Broich, 2006).
	 - Antidepressants often trigger a [[Manic episode]] or [[hypomanic episode]]
### medication adherence
many bipolar patients stop taking their mood stabilizers when they “feel better” (Advokat et al., 2014)
### skills training
- Social skills training and problem-solving skills
