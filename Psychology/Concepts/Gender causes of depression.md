## History:
### middle of the twentieth century: [[Psychodynamic model|Psychodynamic theories]] 
overtook the previous treatments of madness as disability and [[psychosis]]
- examined mental processes and one’s internal psychological workings
- relationships and childhood experience caused difficulties 
- talk therapies were the remedy (Magnusson and Marecek 2012:132–3).
[[Psychiatry]] expanded to offer insights in how to navigate society instead of focusing solely on institutionalizing and treating those deemed mad.
### early 1960s: effective [[Tricyclic antidepressants]]
effectiveness of the newly discovered [[Tricyclic antidepressants]] ->[[Depressive Disorders|depression]] became a manageable disease rather than a ‘‘reactive’’ disorder with causes external to the [[Brain]] (Gordon 2010:49)
### 1970s: era of biomedical psychiatry
[[Psychodynamic model|Psychodynamic theories]] repudiated (rejected) for biomedical approaches and disease models (Magnusson and Marecek 2012:133)
- Allan Horwitz (2002) argues that this re-medicalization was brought about by shifts in the economic, political, and social environments of medicine.
	- apparent efficacy of pharmacological interventions helped in shifting disease models for depression
‘‘chemical imbalance’’ that would later be popularized in the aggressive marketing of Prozac (Gordon 2010:49).
Laura Hirshbein (2009), a psychiatrist and historian of psychiatry, argues that by the 1970s researchers ‘‘worked backward from the presumed effects of [[Antidepressant]] medications to hypothesize about the mechanism of depression’’

Magnusson and Marecek (2012): psychiatry did not go back to institutionalization but ‘‘this collection of experiences became recharacterized as diseases’’
### 1980 Shift toward medicalization: [[Diagnostic and Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders|DSM]] III
- defining psychiatric conditions in the form of a checklist of symptoms (Magnusson and Marecek 2012:134).
- category of ‘reactive’ conditions by **outside** environment
- opposed to ‘‘endogenous’’ conditions with internal causes (Gordon 2010:51)
- blurred the lines between psychological and physical characteristics by placing them in one and the same diagnostic category (Gordon 2010:51)
### 1987: [[Prozac]] on the United States pharmaceutical market
[[Selective serotonin reuptake inhibitors|SSRI]] with fewer side effects than the [[Tricyclic antidepressants]] and could be prescribed to a wider range of patients
- Applbaum (2009) argues that the marketing of Prozac is an instance of [[strategic medicalization]]
- "chemical imbalance" theory dominates -> increasingly endogenous (internal cause) models of psychiatry -> more easily for ignoring gender and other social influences.

## Femininity of [[Depressive Disorders|depression]]
Laura Hirshbein (2009): characteristics deemed ‘feminine,’ as perceived through a lens of patriarchal gender norms and assumptions, actually permeated through concepts of depression from the very beginning.
### circular process
1. depression came from a period in psychiatry where notions of sex (as most aspects of gender were then attributed to biology) overtly grounded their research and disease concepts.
2. Depression then was identified with women, as primarily a disease of women
3. ‘‘patients were defined as depressed because they matched researchers’ assumptions about what depression looked like’’
4. Then women’s predominance in subsequent depression statistics acted to confirm that women were (‘objectively’) depressed more than men (Hirshbein 2009:90).
### clinical treatment and prescription actively downplay the influence of social forces
Power to offer explanations to cause of [[Depressive Disorders|depression]] by authoritative psychiatric knowledge
> [!idea] “How serious is my depression”
- the social influences of depression are denied their reality.
	- if one’s depression has its causes in a certain life event like the death of a loved one or more importantly one’s ‘situation’ like being in poverty or subject to gendered oppression on a regular basis, then it is not the serious kind of depression that requires professional interventions. 
### narrativization of individual mental illness experience
Vanthuyne (2003) describes the three common ‘‘idioms’’ that patients use as a narrative-linguistic template for describing and making sense of their illness experiences:
- psychiatric
- emotional
- political
Magnusson and Marecek (2012) argue that the use of a ‘disease’ metaphor in mental illness implies that one’s psychological suffering is ‘‘an outward manifestation of an underlying internal illness or pathology,as a result there seems to be little need to look for external factors and causes
#### ‘‘psychiatric’’ idiom
impose itself on people’s articulation of their mental health problems
use of narrative structures that the emotional and political idioms provide
- it legitimizes the illness one suffers from
- it allows him or her to ‘get back to normal.’’
relative social acceptance of a legitimate diagnosis; however, there is also the silencing impetus to return to the status quo as quickly as possible.

## Biopsychosocial model
### Comparing depression to [[Bipolar Disorders]]
the rates of bipolar disorder are much more consistent across cultures and sex ratios are nearly equal (Weissman et al. 1996)
- Patriarchy and gender-based oppression arguably are such ‘‘universal social factors’’ and could help explain the variations among cultures not seen in bipolar.
- depression is a matter of power
### "Vulnerability" model of [[Depressive Disorders|depression]]
George Brown and Tirril Harris (1978) *Social Origins of Depression*:
think of vulnerability in both ways—the biomedical and the social—at once.
#### positive influences on vulnerability
reduce vulnerability to depression:
- enduring relationships of emotional and practical support
- qualitative measures of intimacy the predictive factor
#### Negative influences on vulnerability
- the loss of one’s mother before the age of eleven
- having three or more children under fourteen living at home
- lack of employment outside the home
#### "Generalized of hopelessness"
##### Determinants:
Brown and Harris’s (1978) model:
- ongoing self-esteem
- sense of one’s influence over their world/their ability to repair the damage of loss
##### Populations:
- disempowered [[Population]]
- subject to the ‘major stressors’ of severe life events
##### Example:
a woman ‘‘would react to her teenage son’s being picked up by the police with thoughts not just involving this lapse in parenting but also perhaps by generalizing her feelings to include her performance in all her role identities’’

social understandings of depression are less amenable to capital friendly, profit-motivated structures
### Political Factors:
Self-esteem and efficacy linked to one's social influence and control: makes this a political topic:
- relational autonomy (see Mackenzie and Stoljar 2000)
- opportunity and ‘‘equality of opportunity,’’ political recognition (see Fraser and Honneth 2003; Honneth 2004)
- epistemic oppression, silencing, and injustice (see Dotson 2011; Fricker 2007)
