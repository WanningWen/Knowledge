> [!important] a series of recurrent, unexpected panic attacks coupled with the fear of future [[panic attack]]s 
> the attacks are _unexpected_ and _recurrent_
^b15f03
## Diagnosis:
the individual must **present with more than one unexpected full-symptom [[panic attack]]** (APA, 2022).
- **expected panic attacks does not rule out the diagnosis**: [[DSM V|DSM-5-TR]] states about half of individuals diagnosed with the disorder in the United States and Europe have both types of attacks (APA, 2022).
#### [[comorbidity]] with [[Agoraphobia]]
- fear of leaving their home or attending large events occur --> fearful an attack will happen in one of these situations, causing embarrassment
Occasionally, an additional diagnosis of agoraphobia is given to an individual with panic disorder _if_ their behaviors meet diagnostic criteria for this disorder as well.
- individuals report worry that others will think they are “going crazy” or losing control if they were to observe an individual experiencing a [[panic attack]]
## Epidemiology/ Prevalence:
- The 12-month prevalence ^38c4ae
	- general population: around 2-3% in adults and adolescents across the United States and several European countries. 
	- Higher rates found in American Indians and non-Latinx whites
	- 2:1 female to male diagnosis rate. 
	- declines from about 1.2% in adults older than 55 to 0.7% in adults aged 64 and up.
## Comorbidity
- 80% of individuals report symptoms of other [[Anxiety Disorders|anxiety disorders]], [[Major Depressive Disorder|major depressive disorder]], [[Bipolar Disorders|bipolar]] I and bipolar II disorder, and possibly mild alcohol use disorder
- [[Substance Use Disorder]], likely as an attempt to treat their anxiety with alcohol or other substances
- About 25% of patients report suicidal thoughts and the disorder may increase the risk for future suicidal behaviors but not deaths.  (APA, 2022).
- high comorbidity with general medical symptoms:
	- somatic symptoms such as dizziness, cardiac arrhythmias, COPD, asthma, irritable bowel [[syndrome]], and hyperthyroidism (APA, 2022)
## Etiology (Cause)
### Neurobiological structures 
#### **[[locus coeruleus]]** (related but doesn't fully explain)
- individuals with panic disorder may have a hyperactive locus coeruleus, leaving them more susceptible to experience more intense and frequent physiological arousal than the general public (Gorman, Kent, Sullivan, & Coplan, 2000)
- individuals experienced increased panic symptoms following the injection of norepinephrine (Bourin, Malinge, & Guitton, 1995).

## Treatment:
### [[Cognitive Behavioral Therapy|CBT]]:  most effective treatment option
- Nearly 80 percent of people with panic disorder report complete remission of symptoms after mastering the following **five components of CBT for panic disorder** (Craske & Barlow, 2014).
### Medication [[psychoactive medications]]
Statistics:
- 50% patients with panic disorder at psychotherapy are already on medication from their primary care physician. (Craske & Barlow (2014))
- Results from large clinical trials suggest _no advantage_ during or immediately after treatment of combining CBT and medication (Craske & Barlow, 2014).
- when the medication was discontinued post-treatment, the CBT+ medication groups fared worse than the CBT treatment-only groups, thus supporting the theory that immersion in interoceptive exposure is limited due to the use of medication
- medications be reserved for those who do not respond to CBT therapy alone (Kampman, Keijers, Hoogduin & Hendriks, 2002).
