> [!important] perceived defects or flaws in one’s physical appearance _not_ observable or appear slight to others
> run the spectrum from feeling “unattractive” to “looking hideous.”
## Clinical Presentation
- commonly reported areas are skin (acne, wrinkles, skin color), hair (particularly thinning or excessive body hair), and nose (size or shape; APA, 2022)
- can occur _by proxy_ meaning the individual is not concerned with their own defects but those of another person
### Diagnosis:
-  one must spend a considerable amount of time preoccupied with their appearance (i.e., on average 3-8 hours a day), as well as display significant impairment in social, occupational, or other areas of functioning.
- [[DSM V|DSM-5-TR]]: associated with, “abnormalities in emotion recognition, attention, and executive function, as well as information-processing biases and inaccuracies in interpretation of information and social situations” (APA, 2022, pg. 273)
	- indicate the degree of insight regarding body dysmorphic disorder beliefs using _with good or fair insight_, _with poor insight_, or _with absent insight/delusional beliefs_.

 ## Epidemiology/ Prevalence
 - U.S. adults is 2.4% while outside the U.S., the point prevalence is 1.7% to 2.9%.
 - women are more likely
	 - preoccupied with weight, breasts, buttocks, legs, hips, and excessive body or facial hair
 - [[Muscle dysmorphia]] more in men
	 -  genitals, body build, and thinning hair
 ## [[comorbidity]]
 - [[Major Depressive Disorder]] is the most common; occurs after the onset of body dysmorphic disorder
 - some reports of social anxiety disorder, OCD, and substance-related disorders (likely related to muscle enhancement; APA, 2022)
Suicidality:
- four times more likely to have experienced suicidal thoughts and 2.6 times more likely to have made suicide attempts compared to healthy control subjects and those diagnosed with eating disorders, OCD, or any anxiety disorder.

# Treatment 
## [[Mental Health Treatment]]
- [[Exposure and Response Prevention]]  repeatedly exposed to their body imperfections/obsessions and prevented from engaging in compulsions used to reduce their anxiety.  (Veale, Gournay, et al., 1996; Wilhelm, Otto, Lohr, & Deckersbach, 1999).
- [[psychoactive medications|psychopharmacology]]: [[clomipramine]] and [[Selective serotonin reuptake inhibitors|SSRI]]s are generally prescribed
### Difficulty of treatment:
- study finding that only 9% of participants had full remission at a 1-year follow-up, and 21% reported partial remission (Phillips, Pagano, Menard & Stout, 2006)
- A more recent finding reported more promising findings, with 76% of participants reporting full remission over 8 years (Bjornsson, Dyck, et al., 2011).
## Plastic surgery and medical treatments
Phillips and colleagues (2001):
- 76.4% of the patients reported some form of plastic surgery or medical treatment: dermatology treatment the most reported (45%) followed by plastic surgery (23%).
- individual is ==rarely satisfied== with the outcome of the procedure, thus leading them to seek out additional surgeries on the same defect (Phillips et al., 2001)
