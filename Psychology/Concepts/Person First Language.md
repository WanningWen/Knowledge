> [!important] People First Language puts the person before the disability, and describes what a  person *has*, not who a person *is*.
> When we see the diagnosis as the most important characteristic of a person, we  devalue her as an individual.

we make decisions about how/where the person will be educated, whether he’ll work or not,  here/how he’ll live, and what services are offered, ==based on the person’s medical diagnosis, instead of the person’s unique and individual strengths and needs==.

words used about a person have a powerful impact on the person

#Nonprofit/Org/NAMI being aware of how language can shape stereotypes and stigmatizing views about living with mental health conditions

## How:

| Say                                   | Instead of:         |
| ------------------------------------- | ------------------- |
| Living with a mental health challenge | The mentally ill    |
| I have bipolar disorder               | I’m bipolar         |
| His behavior is unusual               | He is acting crazy  |
| She has a mental health condition     | She is mentally ill |
|                                       |                     |
> [!NOTE] Terms such as loony, crazy, wacko, insane, schizo further perpetuate negative stereotypes & stigma

in certain circumstances, and when it’s appropriate, we can==simply share information about what t the person needs== in a respectful, dignified manner, and ==omit the diagnosis==
