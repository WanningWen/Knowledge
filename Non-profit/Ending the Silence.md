# Marilyn's Lived Experiences
[script gdoc link](https://docs.google.com/document/d/1zeYVPecltt2ottv2hPwnM-BQpD29HxWvHPYcOuHbGn0/edit?usp=sharing)
**

Hi, my name is Marilyn. I’m originally from Shanghai, China. I went to high school in Chicago and I was a public health and computer science major at Vanderbilt University in Nashville, Tennessee. Now, I live in Redwood City with my cat, McFluffy. I spend a lot of my time tutoring students in STEM, computer science, and Chinese, and I’m becoming a certified peer specialist. In my free time, I like to go on biking trials and music events.

My mental illness journey started after coming back from the summer break in the junior year of college, I became stuck in my bed all day. I played the same video games over and over again. I felt so exhausted, that getting myself to be barely presentable and hygienic was hard. I thought that anyone who takes a look at me would know I was a total mess. I’ve always been a “good kid”: at boarding school I was in the swim team in high school, the robotics captain, and I went to competitive summer camps during the summer breaks. At first this depressive episode almost felt relieving to me. I felt like this was finally the break I needed to not worry about anything. I just wanted to disappear from all the expectations I needed to meet.  

But after staying in bed for 2 weeks: I barely ate any proper meals, didn’t go to class, never responded to any emails, even when my college dean was concerned. I felt like there was no hope nor a future for myself. I thought no one should even want to see or help me. I thought I was a disappointment to my university, my parents, and every one of my friends. Eventually I was dragged outside of the room by my dorm counselor to urgent care at my college’s counseling center.

There I was first diagnosed with Major Depressive Disorder, GAD and other trauma related disorder. I was relieved when I heard the diagnosis, but I almost felt like I tricked my psychiatrist into giving the diagnosis to me so I could be excused from all the irresponsible actions I’ve done.

 I didn’t think I couldn’t get out of bed because of the Depression, I thought I was just lazy and wanted to watch youtube and play games all day. I thought I didn’t want to see friends because I was faking to be someone I was not. I felt like I was faking this lively and sociable version of myself my entire life. Seeing them would only reveal who I truly am. I felt that, just like how I deceived other people, I convinced my psychiatrist that I have depression. What I didn’t realize at the time was: I was trying to act “OKAY” and lie about how I’m feeling so that I meet other’s expectations for so long, that I thought the diagnosis was just another excuse.

I described it as “I was too ill to realize that I was ill”. I heard this echoed over and over again by peers

To understand what depression does to me was like trying to convince myself that there's an invisible hand controlling my discipline to do basic daily functions. 

I felt like it was just so hard to shower, eat, and talk to people every day. Just doing those basic tasks would exhaust me so much that I couldn’t imagine how I would go to class, do school work, and eventually work a 9-5 job. 

I was so convinced that I had horrible personality flaws that it was hard to think about how these challenges were caused by depression. 

If you can imagine doing everything with a plastic bag over your head and baking gloves as hands, that's what depression was like for me. I felt so incapable, stupid, and dependent on others that I genuinely hated myself. 

At first, I didn’t think therapy or medication could help me. I went through partial hospitalization and then intensive outpatient for a whole semester, doubting everything that was happening. When the therapists would guide us to celebrate small steps of success, like cleaning the room or doing laundry, I remember thinking “why are they treating me like a child”. 

I would beat myself up internally, even when I’m showing signs of recovery. Going as far as to tell myself, on days I do feel better, that these signs of improvement are proof of the fact that I was pretending to have depression. 

I even thought that if I were to tell my therapists the truth: that I was feeling just as bad as a month ago, they would be disappointed and would not want to see me again. I was almost imagining to magically waking up one day, free of depression, and ready to fix all the other problems in my life.

However, along the way, I met peers and listened to their stories of how their illnesses affected them, how they accepted it, and how they healed. Hearing their experiences helped me realize that what I was going through was in fact an illness—it didn’t mean I was weak or lazy, and it could be treated. I learned that when depression takes over, it doesn’t define me. At my best, I am smart, disciplined, and responsible. Instead of thinking that I was faking the “better” version of myself, I learned to accept that when I am depressed, I have a hard time being that version of myself. And that’s okay. It takes time and treatment to be better. 

The most important lesson I learned was to stop running away from depression, be open to treatment, and be honest to people who are close to me. Admitting to the fact that I’m not doing well takes a big mental load off of me. That encourages me to face the illness, and actually focus on getting better. 

For almost 3 years after the diagnosis, I switched between many antidepressant medications to find the one that finally worked for me. I can finally tell the difference between feeling like my brain is cloudy and slow, and when it’s functioning normally. 

Through therapy, I've learned to look out for unhealthy thinking patterns and warning signs for a depressive episode. Laura talked about some of them earlier. One example I experience is: when one of my friends doesn’t text back for a whole day, I start thinking “I must have done something wrong and messed up. I always do this and I can’t maintain any friendships”. This is an all-or-nothing thought and it is not the truth. 

I also learned to watch out for times where I felt like I’m not rested despite over 10 hours of sleep. Those are warning signs that I might be starting a depressive episode. Instead of trying to sleep through the day, I should go outside, talk to some friends, play some music, and apply some other coping skills.

When I was first diagnosed and taking a medical leave from school, my parents completely freaked out. My parents were confused about the cause and effect. Instead of thinking that my depression is affecting how I act and perform at school, they thought that my personality and school life is causing depression. This was especially hard because a lot of my mental illness is caused by childhood trauma. How am I supposed to explain mental health with them when they are part of the cause? It took a lot of work and even teaching and parenting my own parents about mental health to start talking about mental illnesses in ways that actually help me.

When I was in the middle of depression, I felt like I had no way to achieve a good life. Today, I know I still have dreams to chase. I want to finish my college degree, go to graduate school, be a therapist, and eventually get a phd to be a clinical psychologist. 

I’m becoming a certified peer specialist to work in mental health in California. I believe that one step at a time, I will get there. 

I see that, even if the path is difficult and more like an upwards spiral and not a straight path. Instead of expecting myself to be rid of depression entirely and blaming myself for failing at tasks when I feel depressed, I should celebrate myself for the progress I made during recovery. I would not be a version of myself without depression but my future self will be one that recovered from it and learned so much from overcoming the illness.I know that during times when you feel so much difficulties from every aspect of your life, it’s hard to see hope, but I’m a full advocate for there’s always a way out. 

I wanted to share a message that resonated with me a lot: if you’re seeing the next steps to achieving your dreams very clearly and straight, this means you’re on a path someone else paved; When you’re stumbling and trying to dig a way to achieve your goals, that’s how you know you’re on the path that’s meant just for you.
