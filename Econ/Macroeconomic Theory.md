## [[consumption-smoothing motive]]

^749bb9

The desire to have a relatively even pattern of consumption over time—avoiding periods of very high or very low consumption

Y = C + I + G
• Y = total output (GDP)
• C = consumption
• I = investment ^369063

• G = government spending
S = Y - C - G = I

$$S^d = Y - C^d - G$$S: national saving
Y: output
C: consumption
G: government purchases
$S^d$ : desired national saving
$C^d$: desired consumption ^85459e

The ==rate at which Prudence trades off current and future consumptio==n depends on the real interest rate prevailing in the economy. Suppose that Prudence can earn a real interest rate of r per year on her saving and, for simplicity, suppose that if she borrows, she must pay the same real interest rate r on the loan

### Changes in current income
## [marginal propensity to consume], or _MPC_
the fraction of ==additional current income== that she consumes in the current period
- 0 to 1
### Ex: bonus
Suppose, for example that Prudence has an _MPC_ equal to 0.4, so that she consumes 0.4, or 40%, of an increase in current income. Then, when she receives a $6000 bonus, Prudence will increase her current consumption by 0.4×$6000=$2400. Because the part of income that is not consumed is saved, her saving also increases by the amount of $6000−$2400=$3600.

The marginal propensity to consume also applies to _declines_ in current income. For example, if Prudence were temporarily laid off from her bookkeeping job so that her current year’s income decreased by $8000, she would reduce both her consumption and her saving. If we assume that her marginal propensity to consume remains 0.4, she would reduce her consumption by 0.4×$8000=$3200, and her saving would therefore have to diminish by $8000−$3200=$4800.


### Changes in future income:
>[!note] an increase in an individual’s expected future income is likely to lead that person to increase current consumption and decrease current saving

>[!important] If people expect that Y will be higher in the future -- $C^d$  increase and $S^d$ decrease.
#### Ex:
for example, that Prudence decides to consume $1000 more this year. Because her current income is unchanged, Prudence’s $1000 increase in current consumption is equivalent to a $1000 reduction in current saving --> ==reduce Prudence’s available resources in the next year by $1000×(1+r)==. Overall, her available resources next year will increase by $6000 because of the bonus but will decrease by $1050 because of reduced current saving, giving a net increase in resources of $6000−$1050=$4950, which can be used to increase consumption next year or in the following years. Effectively, ==Prudence can use the increase in her expected future income to increase consumption both in the present and in the future.==

### Changes in Wealth:
>[!important] an increase in wealth increases current consumption and reduces current saving. 
>The same line of reasoning leads to the conclusion that a decrease in wealth reduces current consumption and increases saving.


## The [substitution effect of the real interest rate on saving]: ==**current saving increases== in response to an increase in the real interest rate**
the tendency to ==reduce current consumption== and ==increase future consumption== as the price of current consumption $1+r$ increases.
- _substitute_ away from current consumption, which has become relatively more expensive, toward future consumption

## The [income effect of the real interest rate on saving](#P7001015849000000000000000005881) 
reflects the change in current consumption that results when a higher real interest rate makes a consumer richer or poorer
- to ==increase current consumption and reduce current saving==
### For a saver: an increase in the real interest rate --> increase saving through the substitution effect but reduce saving through the income effect
### For Borrower ==both the substitution effect and the income effect of an increase in the real interest rate increase the saving==
The borrower has effectively suffered ==a loss of wealth== as a result of the increase in the real interest rate, and responds to this decline in wealth by ==reducing both current consumption and future consumption== --> current saving increases --> the income effect of an increase in the real interest rate is to ==increase saving==

Because economic theory does not indicate whether national saving increases or decreases in response to an increase in the real interest rate, we must rely on empirical studies. Unfortunately, interpretation of the empirical evidence from the many studies done continues to inspire debate.

widely accepted conclusion seems to be that an increase in the real interest rate reduces current consumption and increases saving, but this effect isn’t very strong


### Expected real after-tax interest rate
the **nominal after-tax interest rate** after payment of taxes:$(1−t)i$ 
- $i$ :the nominal interest rate
- $t$ :the rate at which interest income is taxed
The [expected real after-tax interest rate], $r_a−t$
$$r_a−t=(1−t)i−π^e$$
- $π^e$: the expected inflation rate
>[!important] The expected real after-tax interest rate is the appropriate interest rate for consumers to use in making consumption and saving decisions because it measures the increase in the purchasing power of their saving after payment of taxes.

## Fiscal Policy
>[!important] $C_d$ 小 $S_d$ 小； G小$S_d$小
- we ignore the possibility that the changes in fiscal policy that we consider could affect the aggregate supply of goods and services. This assumption is valid **if the economy is at full employment** and if the fiscal policy changes don’t significantly affect the capital stock or employment.
 raising current taxes or by leading people to expect that taxes will be higher in the future, will cause people to consume less
1. For any levels of Y (output) and G (government purchases), a fiscal policy change that ==reduces $C_d$ ,by $1$ will raise $S_d$, by one dollar==. 
2. For any levels of Y (output) and $C_d$, increases in G directly lower $S_d$:  $S_d=Y−C_d−G$.

#### Government Spending ($G$)
>[!important] $G$ up -->  reduces both desired consumption and desired national saving
>
The increase in government purchases affects desired national saving, $Y−Cd−G$, directly by increasing G and indirectly by reducing desired consumption, $C_d$ , because the decline in desired consumption can be expected to be less than the initial increase in government purchases, ==a temporary increase in government purchases will lower desired national saving==.

changes in government purchases affect consumption because they affect private-sector tax burdens
#### Ex:G up $10 billion in military spending by raising current taxes by $10 billion
For a given total (before-tax) output, Y, this tax increase implies a $10 billion decline in consumers’ current (after-tax) incomes. We know that consumers respond to a decline in their current incomes by reducing consumption, although by less than the decline in current income.[5](#P7001015849000000000000000001E59) So, in response to the $10 billion tax increase, consumers might reduce their current consumption by $6 billion.
